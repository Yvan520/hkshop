Step-by-Step Terrarium Building Guide

Building a terrarium follows a specific sequence. Each layer serves a purpose, and skipping steps leads to mold, rot, or plant death. Here is the correct order:

1. Drainage layer. Start with 2-3 centimeters of pebbles or gravel at the bottom of the glass container. This layer collects excess water and prevents root rot. Without drainage, water pools at the bottom and creates anaerobic conditions that kill plants.

2. Charcoal layer. Sprinkle a thin layer of activated charcoal over the pebbles. Charcoal absorbs impurities, prevents odors, and inhibits fungal growth. This layer is non-negotiable for closed terrariums where air circulation is limited.

3. Soil layer. Add 4-6 centimeters of sterile potting mix. A mix designed for succulents or tropical plants works best. Avoid garden soil, which contains pests and compacts too densely for container growing.

4. Hardscape. Arrange stones, driftwood, or bark to create the structural skeleton of your landscape. This is the stage where you decide the visual flow: mountains on one side, open space on the other, a path through the middle.

5. Moss carpet. Lay sheet moss or cushion moss over the soil. Moss retains moisture, prevents soil erosion during watering, and provides the lush green ground cover that makes terrariums visually appealing. Press it gently into the soil.

6. Plants. Choose small plants that tolerate high humidity. Ferns, fittonia, peperomia, and pilea are excellent choices. Use tweezers to plant them without disturbing the moss layer. Leave space between plants for growth.

7. Figurines. This is where personality enters the terrarium. Miniature resin figurines such as mushrooms, animals, or tiny furniture pieces create narrative scenes. Xiaohongshu users love themed terrariums with forest spirits, reading nooks, or camping scenes.

8. LED ring light. Most complete DIY terrarium kits include a USB-powered LED ring that sits on top or inside the container. The light serves dual purpose: it provides the energy plants need for photosynthesis and illuminates the scene as a night light.

Maintenance Tips

Closed terrariums require very little care. Water once every two to four weeks, depending on condensation levels. If the glass is consistently foggy, reduce watering frequency. Place the terrarium in bright, indirect light. Direct sunlight through glass creates a greenhouse effect that can cook the plants inside. Prune any leaves that touch the glass, as they will rot from condensation contact.
